🌿 Garden District Walking Tour

New Orleans — Saturday, April 18, 2026

~1.5 mi loop · 90 min fast / 2.5 hrs with stops

🚋 Getting There

Board the St. Charles streetcar going uptown at Carondelet & Canal. $1.25 exact change or Le Pass app. Sit on the right for mansion views. Ride ~22 min, get off at Washington Ave — you'll spot Commander's Palace turquoise paint as your cue to pull the cord.

The Walk
1

Commander's Palace & Lafayette Cemetery

HistoricDining
1403 Washington Ave & 1400 Washington Ave
Commander's Palace — the turquoise Victorian that's impossible to miss. Built 1893, this is where Emeril Lagasse and Paul Prudhomme launched their careers before becoming household names. 7x James Beard Award winner. The turquoise color isn't accidental — it's been the restaurant's signature since the Brennan family took over in 1974. Saturday Jazz Brunch runs 11 AM - 2 PM with a live trio.

Lafayette Cemetery No. 1 — across the street. The tombs are above ground because New Orleans sits below sea level — bury someone and they float back up. Anne Rice held her own mock funeral here in 1995 to celebrate a book launch, complete with horse-drawn hearse and jazz band. Featured in Interview with the Vampire and Double Jeopardy. If the gate's locked (common during restoration), peek through the iron fence — the view is still worth it.
2

Brevard-Rice House (Rosegate)

ArchitectureLiterary
1239 First Street · 1857
One of the finest Greek Revival houses in the district, built in 1857 for cotton merchant Albert Hamilton Brevard. The pink-and-gray palette, the ornamental wrought iron fence, and the deep setback behind magnolias are all signature Garden District design — wealthy Americans building their own neighborhood upriver from the French Creoles, importing East Coast and European architectural styles and scaling them up to announce their arrival.

Architectural detail: The proportions are textbook Greek Revival — symmetrical facade, tall columns, raised gallery — but the ironwork and lush landscaping are distinctly New Orleans. This fusion of Northern formalism and Southern ornament defines the district.

Literary note: Anne Rice set her Mayfair Witches trilogy at this exact address. The house became a literary landmark — a rare case where fiction and architecture are permanently fused to a specific building.
3

Morris-Israel House

Architecture
1331 First Street
Italianate masterpiece, 1869. Look up at the cast-iron galleries on the second floor — the filigree detail is extraordinary. This style of ironwork is unique to New Orleans at this concentration. The Garden District was built by Anglo-American merchants who deliberately competed with each other in architectural grandeur. Each mansion was a statement: I've arrived, and I have taste. The ironwork here is among the finest examples of that one-upmanship.
4

First Street Mansions — The Rivalry Block

ArchitectureHistoric
1400 block of First Street · 1850s-1870s
First Street between Prytania and Coliseum is the single densest concentration of antebellum mansions in the district. What you're seeing is a block-by-block arms race: cotton merchants, sugar planters, and commission agents each hiring the same small pool of architects — Henry Howard, James Gallier Jr., Lewis Reynolds — and demanding something grander than the neighbor's.

Why it matters: The Garden District was the "American sector." After the Louisiana Purchase in 1803, Anglo-Americans flooded into New Orleans but weren't welcome in the old French Quarter. They built their own city upriver — bigger, showier, and deliberately distinct from Creole architecture. These houses are that ambition made physical. Notice how they sit back from the street behind gardens, unlike the French Quarter's flush-to-sidewalk Creole townhouses. That setback was an intentional class signal: I have land to waste.
5

Coliseum Street — Greek Revival Row

Architecture
2400 block of Coliseum Street · 1850s-1860s
Coliseum is narrower, leafier, and quieter than First — this is the residential heart of the district. The houses here are classic Greek Revival: white columned porticoes, symmetrical facades, temple-front pediments. This style dominated American architecture from the 1830s to the 1860s, and the Garden District has one of the best surviving collections in the country.

Why Greek Revival here? The Anglo-Americans who built these houses saw themselves as building a new democratic civilization — and borrowed the visual language of ancient Athens and Rome to say so. The irony, of course, is that this wealth was built on enslaved labor and the cotton economy. The columns are beautiful and the history is brutal, and both things are true at the same time.

Look for: The live oak canopy overhead. These trees are 150+ years old, their roots buckling the sidewalks. The oaks and the houses grew up together.
6

Women's Opera Guild House

Architecture
2507 Coliseum Street
Built 1858, also called the Davis House. One of the few Garden District mansions you can actually tour inside — most are private. Greek Revival with Italianate touches. The Women's Opera Guild has maintained it since 1944, preserving the original furnishings, artwork, and decor. If it's open, step in — you'll see what these places look like behind the hedges and iron fences.
7

The Nolan House

Architecture
2707 Coliseum Street · 1869
Stand across the street — you need the distance to take in the scale. Double-gallery wraparound porches on both floors, white, enormous, set behind a deep hedge. Built 1869, this is one of the finest examples of Italianate-influenced antebellum architecture in the South.

Architectural detail: The double gallery (two-story wraparound porch) is a distinctly Southern solution to the climate — it shades the interior, catches cross-breezes, and creates an outdoor living space on the upper floor that functions as a second parlor. The scale here is extraordinary even by Garden District standards. Notice the paired columns, the ornamental brackets under the eaves, and how the house addresses the corner — it was designed to be seen from multiple angles, not just the front.

Historical context: Built just four years after the Civil War ended, when the cotton economy that funded these mansions had collapsed. This house represents the last gasp of antebellum grandeur — constructed with the old aesthetic but in a fundamentally changed world.
8

Chestnut Street — The Quiet Interior

Architecture
Fourth & Chestnut Street area
This detour off the main tour route gives you the Garden District without the tour groups. Chestnut and the surrounding blocks are quieter, more residential — people gardening, kids on porches, dogs on walks.

What to notice: The shift from grand mansions to more modest (but still beautiful) Creole cottages and shotgun houses as you move away from the district's center. The shotgun house — rooms lined up front to back with no hallway — is one of New Orleans' most distinctive architectural forms, likely descended from West African and Haitian building traditions brought through the Caribbean. You'll see them in every New Orleans neighborhood, but here they sit just blocks from 22,000-square-foot cotton baron mansions. The proximity tells the whole story of the city's class structure.
9

Buckner Mansion

ArchitectureHistoric
1410 Jackson Avenue · 1856
Stop and take this in. Arguably the grandest mansion in the entire district — genuinely looks like it shouldn't be real. Twenty-two columns. 22,000 square feet. Built 1856 for cotton baron Henry Buckner, who specifically told his architect to build something bigger than his rival's house down the street.

Architectural significance: This is Greek Revival at its most ambitious and unrestrained. The facade is a full-width temple front with paired Corinthian columns on both stories — a scale normally reserved for public buildings, not private homes. The message was deliberately civic: Buckner wanted his house to look like an institution. And it eventually became one — serving as Soulé Business College for nearly a century.

Historical context: This is what "new money in 1850s cotton" looked like. The cotton economy made New Orleans the wealthiest city in the antebellum South, and the Garden District was where that wealth was performed in architecture. Buckner's mansion is the ultimate expression of that impulse — scale as social proof, columns as currency.
The Transition

Garden District → Magazine Street

From Buckner, walk back to Prytania, then cut southwest on Third Street toward the river. You'll cross Coliseum, then Camp, then hit Magazine. Watch the architecture shift: the grand mansions give way, one block at a time, to Creole cottages and shotgun houses, then to the commercial strip. You're watching the class structure of 1850s New Orleans in architecture — wealth radiating outward from the river, the grandest homes nearest the high ground.
Magazine Street Shopping
10

Magazine Street — The Six-Mile Strip

Shopping
Magazine St from Jackson Ave to Louisiana Ave (~15 blocks)
Magazine Street is New Orleans' answer to Brooklyn's Atlantic Ave or LA's Abbott Kinney — six miles of independent boutiques, antique shops, galleries, and cafés. The best stretch for this walk runs from Jackson to Louisiana Ave (~15 blocks). Don't try to see it all — pick a side and work it slowly.

What to look for: Vintage clothing shops, local art galleries, antique stores with estate jewelry, locally made hot sauce and pralines, indie bookstores. The vibe is unhurried and local — no chain stores, no rush. This is where New Orleanians actually shop.

Good coffee stop: Look for any place with a line of locals outside. That's your signal.
