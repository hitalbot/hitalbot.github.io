🌿 Garden District Walking Tour

New Orleans — Saturday, April 18, 2026

~1.5 mi loop · 90 min fast / 2.5 hrs with stops

🚋 Getting There

Board the St. Charles streetcar going uptown at Carondelet & Canal. $1.25 exact change or Le Pass app. Sit on the right for mansion views. Ride ~22 min, get off at Washington Ave — you'll spot Commander's Palace turquoise paint as your cue to pull the cord.

The Walk
1

Commander's Palace & Lafayette Cemetery

HistoricDining
1403 Washington Ave & 1400 Washington Ave
Commander's Palace — the turquoise Victorian that's impossible to miss. Built 1893, this is where Emeril Lagasse and Paul Prudhomme launched their careers before becoming household names. 7x James Beard Award winner. The turquoise color isn't accidental — it's been the restaurant's signature since the Brennan family took over in 1974. Saturday Jazz Brunch runs 11 AM - 2 PM with a live trio.

Lafayette Cemetery No. 1 — across the street. The tombs are above ground because New Orleans sits below sea level — bury someone and they float back up. Anne Rice held her own mock funeral here in 1995 to celebrate a book launch, complete with horse-drawn hearse and jazz band. Featured in Interview with the Vampire and Double Jeopardy. If the gate's locked (common during restoration), peek through the iron fence — the view is still worth it.
2

Anne Rice's House (Rosegate)

CelebrityLiterary
1239 First Street
Pink and gray Greek Revival, 1857, behind a wrought iron fence and magnolias. Anne Rice lived here from 1989 to 2004 and set her Mayfair Witches trilogy at this exact address — the "Witching Hour" house. Every Halloween she threw a legendary costume party with guests stretching down the block. When she sold it, she said she was done with New Orleans. She came back. Originally built for Albert Brevard, a cotton merchant. This is the single most photographed literary landmark in the Garden District.
3

Morris-Israel House

Architecture
1331 First Street
Italianate masterpiece, 1869. Look up at the cast-iron galleries on the second floor — the filigree detail is extraordinary. This style of ironwork is unique to New Orleans at this concentration. The Garden District was built by Anglo-American merchants who deliberately competed with each other in architectural grandeur. Each mansion was a statement: I've arrived, and I have taste. The ironwork here is among the finest examples of that one-upmanship.
4

The Manning House

Celebrity
1420 First Street
This is where Archie, Olivia, Cooper, Peyton, and Eli Manning lived. Peyton and Eli grew up throwing footballs on this lawn — the same lawn that produced two Super Bowl MVPs. Sold in 2002. No plaque, no fanfare. New Orleans is famously cool about its celebrities — people live here, not perform here. The Mannings are still deeply embedded in the city's identity. Archie was the Saints' first draft pick in 1971, arriving in a city that had never had a winning season.
5

Sandra Bullock's House

Celebrity
2425 Coliseum Street
White Greek Revival columns, classic Garden District elegance. Bullock bought this in 2009 and is one of New Orleans' most committed celebrity residents. After Hurricane Katrina in 2005, she donated $1 million to the Red Cross and has been deeply involved in rebuilding efforts, particularly through the Warren Easton Charter High School. She's regularly spotted at local restaurants and grocery stores — a genuine New Orleanian, not a part-timer.
6

Women's Opera Guild House

Architecture
2507 Coliseum Street
Built 1858, also called the Davis House. One of the few Garden District mansions you can actually tour inside — most are private. Greek Revival with Italianate touches. The Women's Opera Guild has maintained it since 1944, preserving the original furnishings, artwork, and decor. If it's open, step in — you'll see what these places look like behind the hedges and iron fences.
7

The Benjamin Button House

Film LocationHistoric
2707 Coliseum Street
Wraparound porches on both floors, white, enormous, set back behind a hedge. This is where Brad Pitt's character grew up in the 2008 film The Curious Case of Benjamin Button. Officially the Nolan House (sometimes called Elms Mansion), built 1869. The most-photographed private house in the neighborhood. Stand across the street for the full view — you need the distance to take in the scale. The house predates the movie by 140 years and is genuinely one of the finest examples of antebellum architecture in the South.
8

John Goodman's Quiet Block

Celebrity
Fourth Street & Chestnut Street area
John Goodman lives quietly in this pocket of the Garden District — he actually walks his dog and is genuinely part of the neighborhood. Don't hunt for the exact address; respect the privacy. This detour through Chestnut gives you a few blocks of the quieter, residential Garden District away from the tour groups. You'll see day-to-day mansion life — people gardening, kids on porches, dogs. This is what the neighborhood actually feels like when the walking tours aren't passing through.
9

Buckner Mansion

Film/TVHistoric
1410 Jackson Avenue
Stop and take this in. Built 1856 for cotton baron Henry Buckner, who specifically told his architect to build something bigger than his rival's house down the street. Twenty-two columns. 22,000 square feet. It served as Soulé College for a century before becoming a private residence again. You know it as Miss Robichaux's Academy for Exceptional Young Ladies from American Horror Story: Coven. It's arguably the grandest mansion in the district — genuinely looks like it shouldn't be real. The scale is almost absurd, which was exactly the point. This is what "new money in 1850s cotton" looked like.
The Transition

Garden District → Magazine Street

From Buckner, walk back to Prytania, then cut southwest on Third Street toward the river. You'll cross Coliseum, then Camp, then hit Magazine. Watch the architecture shift: the grand mansions give way, one block at a time, to Creole cottages and shotgun houses, then to the commercial strip. You're watching the class structure of 1850s New Orleans in architecture — wealth radiating outward from the river, the grandest homes nearest the high ground.
Magazine Street Shopping
10

Magazine Street — The Six-Mile Strip

Shopping
Magazine St from Jackson Ave to Louisiana Ave (~15 blocks)
Magazine Street is New Orleans' answer to Brooklyn's Atlantic Ave or LA's Abbott Kinney — six miles of independent boutiques, antique shops, galleries, and cafés. The best stretch for this walk runs from Jackson to Louisiana Ave (~15 blocks). Don't try to see it all — pick a side and work it slowly.

What to look for: Vintage clothing shops, local art galleries, antique stores with estate jewelry, locally made hot sauce and pralines, indie bookstores. The vibe is unhurried and local — no chain stores, no rush. This is where New Orleanians actually shop.

Good coffee stop: Look for any place with a line of locals outside. That's your signal.
